Chapter Six: Child Nutrition

Chapter Description

This chapter describes the nutritional assessment and counseling; nutritional requirements, optimal breastfeeding, complementary feeding, dietary diversity, micronutrient supplementation, nutrition sensitive interventions and implementation modalities for children under ten years of age.


Learning Objective

After completing this chapter, participants will be able to demonstrate the knowledge, attitude, and skills required to provide quality under ten years of age children nutritional interventions and services.


Enabling Objectives

  • Describe the importance of nutrition for children under ten years of age.

  • Describe the nutritional requirements for children under ten years of age.

  • Describe optimal infant and young child feeding (IYCF) practices under normal and in special situations.

  • Identify child nutrition services and their implementation modalities.

  • Explain how to integrate nutrition with key sectors and programs.

    1. Optimal Infant and Young Child Nutrition Practices


      Optimal IYCN practices during the first two years are critical to breaking the cycle of malnutrition from generation to generation. The first 24 months of a child‘s life is recognized as being the most important window of opportunity for establishing healthy growth.


      Baby Friendly Health facility Initiative

      The Ten Steps of Baby Friendly Health facility Initiative (BFHI) is a global quality improvement program launched in 1991 by the WHO and UNICEF, in response to the 1990 Innocenti Declaration on the Protection, Promotion and Support of Breastfeeding.

      The BFHI has been recognized for its potential to have a significant influence on the success of breastfeeding initiation, long-term breastfeeding duration, and increased breastfeeding exclusivity rates. BFHI aims to establish breastfeeding as the norm and ensure that every baby receives the best start in life. Breastfeeding has a major positive effect on the health and wellbeing of infants and children, mothers, and society.

      1. Infant and Young Child Feeding Problems in Ethiopia


        Optimal IYCN practices are the most effective interventions to improve child health and survival. It is estimated that sub-optimal breastfeeding, in the first 6 months of life, results in 1.4 million deaths and 10% of the disease burden in children under five years (OECD/WHO 2012: breastfeeding in health at a glance).


        Common IYCF Problems in Ethiopia are:

         Delayed initiation of breastfeeding

         Giving pre-lacteal feeds (water, milk, or other liquids by bottles) in place of colostrum

         Premature or delayed introduction of complementary foods

         Dilute or watery starchy foods with low nutrient density

         Insufficient dietary diversity, especially of animal source foods

         Low frequency of feeding

         Fear of children choking or being unable to digest meat or thick foods.

         Inadequate amounts consumed per meal (small servings, lack of supervision, lack of appetite)

         Inadequate responsive feeding practices

         Lack of persuasion during child feeding

         Reducing or stopping breastfeeding or complementary feeding during child illness

         Stopping breastfeeding during pregnancy


      2. Why Does Optimal Infant and Young Child Nutrition Matter?


        The consequences of unmet nutritional deficiencies in a child‘s early life are identified to cause higher infant and child mortality, increased susceptibility to infection and illness, reduced adult physical stature, impaired cognitive abilities, and long-term loss in productivity, with economic losses to individuals and society. In Ethiopia, even though the prevalence of stunting reduced from 52% in 2000 to 39% in 2023, stunting is still the highest in Africa and demands a focused attention.

        • Mothers‘ breastfeeding their babies is one of the best ways to ensure a child‘s health and survival. Breast milk gives infants the nutrients they need for healthy development, including the antibodies that help protect them from common childhood illnesses such as diarrhea and pneumonia, the two primary causes of child mortality worldwide.

        • Improvements of complementary feeding have been shown to be most effective to improve child growth, and thereby, to contribute to reducing stunting.


          Key IYCN messages during the first 1,000 days (window of opportunity)


          Each window has specific IYCN services starting with a woman‘s pregnancy up to her child‘s second birthday which is known to be critical to the future health, wellbeing, and success of her child.

          The first 1,000 days of life is a ―critical window‘ of opportunity for implementing nutrition interventions for children under 2 years to ensure optimal child nutrition.

          The right nutrition during the first 1,000 days is known to have a profound impact on a child‘s ability to grow, learn and develop to the full potential.

          How are IYCN and early childhood development related?

          The period from birth to about 36 months is known to be a critical period in early childhood development (ECD) for stimulating positive cognitive development, particularly in settings where ill health and under nutrition are common.

          Interaction with the infant during breastfeeding and responsive feeding during complementary feeding are known to provide constant positive stimulation from the mother and child who can contribute to emotional and psychological development of infants. There is also strong evidence of higher performance in intelligence tests, increase educational attainment and income during adulthood (Lancet 2015). Cognitive stimulation through early childhood development is an important intervention to tackle stunting, iodine deficiency and iron deficiency.


          Early childhood development and its importance

          Child development is the period from conception to primary school entry age (0-8 years). Early Childhood development (ECD) consists of integrated services such as good health, balanced nutrition, safety and security of child, clean and good parent-child interaction with lots of play and communication. Ensuring children to access these services will enable them to be healthy, perform well in school, like school with reduced repetition of class, complete school to job, and become a good citizen, and will be able to take care of his/her own family. From conception to three years of life are very important in the provision of nurturing care because more than 80% of brain develops during this time and is a critical period. ECD could be promoted during GMP, VAC supplementation, deworming, cooking demonstrations, nutritional screening and other nutrition services.


          Providing responsive care will foster early child development. Children‘s brains develop fastest in the first 3 years of life. Observing children, trying to understand them, responding to their signals, needs and interests and letting children explore things around them and talking to them daily is what helps their brains develop. When children make something, make some gestures or sounds, responding to his/her need; and as the child does something again, parents‘ response to their needs is called responsive care. Responsive care is the key to helping our children develop.

      1. Recommended IYCN practices for 0-6-Month Infants


Delayed cord clamping - WHO recommends delayed cord clamping as part of the essential newborn care services. Delayed cord clamping to 2-3 minutes or until cord pulsation ceases allow a physiological transfer of placental blood to the infant, the majority of which occurs within 3 min. Hence, this placental transfusion provides sufficient iron reserves and can improve the infant‘s iron status for up to 6 months. Birth weight measurement - Birth weight should be measured immediately after delivery to assure that the neonate is in the acceptable weight range. If a baby is diagnosed with low birth weight (LBW), it is mandatory to provide or refer to appropriate care and feeding. Measure birth weight immediately after birth; provide appropriate care and feeding. If a referral is available refer for appropriate care. Counsel the mother on delaying bathing for 24 hours after birth.

Skin to skin contact – Support practical bonding between the mother and the infant through touching of their skin. Dry the infant and place directly on the bare chest of the mother and both are covered with a worm cloth. Immediate skin to skin contact is advised to improve survival of small and preterm babies. It is scientifically proven to be one of the best techniques for stimulating the baby to move to the mother‘s breast and attach to let it start feeding upon her breast.

Early initiation of breastfeeding – Support mothers to initiate breastfeeding within one hour of birth. It protects the newborn from acquiring infection, reduces newborn mortality, facilitates emotional bonding of the mother and the baby, and has a positive impact on duration of exclusive breastfeeding. It also prevents postpartum hemorrhage and increases breast milk production.

Giving colostrum - It is a nutrient-dense milk which contains antibodies that protect the newborn against disease. It has high protein, low fat and sugar, high level of secretory immunoglobulin A (SIgA) and helps to expel meconium natural laxative.

Avoid pre-lacteal feeding - Pre-lacteal feeding is giving any semi-solid or liquid foods other than breast milk during the first three days after birth. It affects timely initiation of breastfeeding and exclusive breastfeeding practices. Furthermore, pre-lacteal feeding is a source of infection and interferes with establishing breastfeeding practices.

Optimal breastfeeding – is a comprehensive concept which includes skin to skin contact, early initiation of breastfeeding within one hour of birth, feeding of colostrum, exclusive breastfeeding for the first six months of age, frequent breastfeeding day and night, increase frequency of breastfeeding during and after illness and continue breastfeeding for the first two years of age and beyond.


Facts about breastfeeding

The size of a woman‘s breast does not affect how much milk she can make. Even women who are sick or thin can make enough milk for their baby.

Characteristics of Breast milk

  • Breast milk is always clean

  • Breast milk is always ready and at the right temperature.

  • Breast milk is easy to digest.

  • Nutrients are easily absorbed from breast milk.

  • Breast milk protects against allergies.

  • Breast milk antibodies protect the baby‘s gut by preventing harmful substances from passing into the blood.

  • Breast milk contains enough water for the baby‘s needs.

  • Breast milk has a low protein content which makes it suitable for feeding small infants before their kidneys are fully developed. The amount of protein is adequate to promote the normal growth of the baby.

  • Breast milk is low in saturated fatty acids; saturated fatty acids from cow‘s milk may

The oxytocin reflex – When a baby suckles at the breast, the tongue and the mouth touch the nipple. The nerves in the nipple send a message to the mother‘s brain that the baby wants milk. The brain responds and tells the body to make the milk flow for this feed and to make milk for the next feed. The more the baby suckles, the more milk is produced. How a mother feels and what she thinks can affect how her milk flows. If a woman is happy and confident that she can breastfeed, her milk flows well. But if she doubts whether she can breastfeed, her worries may stop the milk from flowing.

Good positioning and attachment

POSITIONING

The recommended positioning for breast feeding the baby is: -

  • Mother should be sitting (or lying) somewhere comfortable, so she is relaxed. If it helps, she can support a baby on a cushion/pillow.

  • Baby should be facing the breast.

  • Baby and mother should be stomach to stomach.

  • The baby‘s stomach should be against the mother‘s stomach.

  • Baby‘s back and head should be in a straight line.

  • Mother should bring the baby to the breast, not her breast to the baby.

  • Hold the baby at the back of his shoulders—not the back of his head. Be careful not to push the baby‘s head forward.

  • The baby‘s head and body should be in a straight line. A baby cannot suckle or swallow easily if his head is twisted or bent.

  • The baby‘s whole body should be supported with the mother‘s arm along the baby‘s back. This is particularly important for newborns and young babies. For older babies, support of the upper part of the body is usually enough. A mother needs to be careful about using the hand of the same arm, which supports her baby's back, to hold his bottom. Holding his bottom may result in her pulling him too far out to the side, so that his head is in the crook (bend) of her arm. He then must bend his head forward to reach the nipple, which makes it difficult for him to suckle.

    Bad positioning and attachment could result in some problems for the baby and mother in the first couple of months. The baby does not get enough milk, which can cause the baby to grow poorly or the mother‘s breasts to become engorged; the mother will be uncomfortable or in pain. Breast sores among HIV positive mothers can increase the risk of transmission.

    Signs of good positioning

     Infant‘s whole body is in straight line and facing the mother (breast) and close to her.

     Mother holds the infant‘s entire body, not just the neck and shoulders.

    For proper attachment, a baby should take the nipple deeply with mouth open wide, and more of the areola should be seen above the baby‘s mouth than below.


    Having an effective attachment will maximize an effective milk transfer from mother to baby, which in turn will ensure the infant gets adequate breast milk. Attaching correctly will also avoid your nipples becoming sore and cracked and will ensure the milk ducts are being drained effectively, which will avoid blocked milk ducts and breast engorgement.


    How to attach your baby for breastfeeding:

  • Ensure that you and your baby are in a good position and that the baby is lined up nose to nipple. His/her neck is free to tilt back to reach up for your nipple.

  • Wait until baby opens his/her mouth wide, then bring baby towards you – not you towards baby. You want your baby to take a good mouthful of breast underneath the areola (the brown area) as well as the nipple, for your baby to feed effectively.

  • Baby‘s lower lip and chin touch your breast first, so he/she can scoop underneath of the breast below the areola (the brown area), then the rest of the breast will follow into your baby‘s mouth. The nipple will extend to the back of the baby‘s mouth, sitting on the soft part of the roof of his/her mouth.

      1. Reasons to express breast milk

        There are conditions in which a mother needs to express the breast milk to fulfill the nutritional need of the baby. All mothers should learn how to express their milk, so that they know how to do it if needed.

        Breast milk can be stored for about eight hours at room temperature (or up to 24 hours in a refrigerator).

        There are many reasons why a woman may express breast milk:

        • Leave breast milk for a baby when his mother goes out or goes to work.

        • Feed a low-birth-weight baby who cannot suckle.

        • Feed a sick baby, who cannot suckle enough.

        • Keep up the supply of breast milk when a mother or baby is ill.

        • Prevent leaking when a mother is away from her baby.

        • Help a baby to attach to a full breast.

        • Help with breast health conditions (engorgement).


          In advising the mother to express her breast milk, the following points needs to be taken into consideration:

          • Hand expression is the most useful way to express milk. It needs no appliance, so a woman can do it anywhere, at any time.

          • The breasts are easily hurt if another person tries.

          • It is important to prepare a container for the expressed breast milk - a cup, glass, jug, or jar with a wide mouth will work:

          • Wash the cup in soap and boiling water and put in clean place with cover.
